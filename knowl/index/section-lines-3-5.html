We already have a symbol, \(m\text{,}\) for the slope of a line. That other feature, where the line crosses the \(y\)-axis is of interest to us now. The \(y\)-intercept of a line is a point where the line crosses the \(y\)-axis. Since it’s on the \(y\)-axis, the \(x\)-coordinate of this point is \(0\text{.}\) It is standard to call the point \((0,b)\) the \(y\)-intercept, and call the number \(b\) the “\(y\)-coordinate of the \(y\)-intercept”. People will find this too wordy, and will call \(b\) the \(y\)-intercept. But technically, the \(y\)-intercept is \((0,b)\text{.}\)
