Parts of a Whole.

Fractions are parts of a whole.
🔗
In Figure 1, we see \(1\) whole divided into \(7\) equal parts. Since \(3\) parts are shaded, we have an illustration of the fraction “three sevenths”, written \(\frac{3}{7}\text{.}\) The denominator \(7\) tells us how many parts to cut up the whole; since we have \(7\) equal parts, they’re called “sevenths”. The numerator \(3\) tells us how many of those sevenths we have.
🔗

Checkpoint A.1.2. A Fraction as Parts of a Whole.

To visualize the fraction \(\frac{14}{35}\text{,}\) you might cut a rectangle into equal parts, and then count up of them.
🔗
Explanation.
You could cut a rectangle into \(35\) equal pieces, and then \(14\) of them would represent \(\frac{14}{35}\text{.}\)
🔗

We can also locate fractions on a number line. When ticks are equally spread apart, as in Figure 3, each tick represents a fraction.

Checkpoint A.1.4. A Fraction on a Number Line.

In the given number line, what fraction is marked?
🔗
Explanation.
There are \(8\) subdivisions between \(0\) and \(1\text{,}\) and the mark is at the fifth subdivision. So the mark is \(\frac{5}{8}\) of the way from \(0\) to \(1\) and therefore represents the fraction \(\frac{5}{8}\text{.}\)

Checkpoint A.1.5. A Fraction on a Number Line.

Find the fraction \(\frac79\) on the numberline.
🔗
Explanation.
There are \(9\) subdivisions between \(0\) and \(1\text{.}\) Place a mark at the seventh one.
