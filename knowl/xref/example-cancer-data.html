Example 1.3.10.

The following data, given in both table and graphed form, gives the counts of cancer diagnoses in Oregon over a period of time. (oregon.gov/oha/ph/diseasesconditions/chronicdisease/datareports/pages/cancer-incidence.aspx)
🔗

Note the drop in 2020 is possibly explained by under-diagnosing, when people were in COVID quarantine at home and it was difficult to see a doctor for things like a cancer screening.
🔗
What was the rate of change in Oregon cancer diagnoses between 2007 and 2010? The total (net) change in diagnoses over that timespan is
\begin{equation*} 19861 - 19417 = 444 \end{equation*}
meaning that there were \(444\) more cancer incidents in 2010 than in 2007. Since \(3\) years passed (which you can calculate as \(2010-2007\)), the rate of change is \(444\) diagnoses per \(3\) years, or
\begin{equation*} \frac{444\,\text{diagnoses}}{3\,\text{year}}=148\,\frac{\text{diagnoses}}{\text{year}} \end{equation*}
We read that last quantity as “\(148\) diagnoses per year”. This rate of change means that between the years \(2007\) and \(2010\text{,}\) there were \(148\) more diagnoses each year, on average. This is just an average over those three years—it does not mean that the diagnoses grew by exactly this much each year.
