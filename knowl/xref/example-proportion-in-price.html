Example 1.1.15.

Suppose we know the cost of a \(21\)-ounce box of cereal, and we want to know the cost of and \(18\)-ounce box assuming that it costs the same per ounce. Letting \(C\) be this unknown cost (in dollars), we could set up a proportional equation. Whether we are dealing with the smaller box or the larger box, the ratio of cost to weight is supposed to be the same. So in the following equation on the left, we enter details for the larger box. And on the right, for the smaller box.
\begin{align*} \frac{\text{cost in dollars}}{\text{weight in oz}}\amp=\frac{\text{cost in dollars}}{\text{weight in oz}}\\ \frac{\$3.99}{21\,\text{oz}}\amp=\frac{\$C}{18\,\text{oz}} \end{align*}
We can rewrite this equation without the units:
\begin{equation*} \frac{3.99}{21}=\frac{C}{18} \end{equation*}
And we have an equation. At this time, we are still only interested in setting up equations, so we are not concerned with solving this equation.
