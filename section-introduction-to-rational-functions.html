    Section 3.1 Introduction to Rational Functions

    In this chapter we will learn about rational functions, which are ratios of two polynomial functions. Creating this ratio inherently requires division, and we‚Äôll explore the effect this has on the graphs of rational functions and their domain and range.

    Subsection 3.1.1 Graphs of Rational Functions

    Definition 3.1.6. Rational Function.

    A rational function \(f\) is a function in the form
    \begin{equation*} f(x)=\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)} \end{equation*}
    where \(P\) and \(Q\) are polynomial functions, but \(Q\) is not the constant zero function.

    Example 3.1.8.

    Build a table and sketch the graph of the function \(f\) where \(f(x)=\frac{1}{x-2}\text{.}\) Find the function‚Äôs domain and range.
    üîó
    Since \(x=2\) makes the denominator \(0\text{,}\) the function will be undefined for \(x=2\text{.}\) We‚Äôll start by choosing various \(x\)-values and plotting the associated points.
    üîó
    Note that extra points were chosen near \(x=2\) in the Figure¬†9, but it‚Äôs still not clear on the graph what happens really close to \(x=2\text{.}\) It will be essential that we include at least one \(x\)-value between \(1\) and \(2\) and also between \(2\) and \(3\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Further, we‚Äôll note that dividing one number by a number that is close to \(0\) yields a large number. For example, \(\frac{1}{0.0005}=2000\text{.}\) In fact, the smaller the number is that we divide by, the larger our result becomes. So when \(x\) gets closer and closer to \(2\text{,}\) then \(x-2\) gets closer and closer to \(0\text{.}\) And then \(\frac{1}{x-2}\) takes very large values.
    üîó
    When we plot additional points closer and closer to \(2\text{,}\) we get larger and larger results. To the left of \(2\text{,}\) the results are negative, so the connected curve has an arrow pointing downward there. The opposite happens to the right of \(x=2\text{,}\) and an arrow points upward. We‚Äôll also draw the vertical line \(x=2\) as a dashed line to indicate that the graph never actually touches it.
    üîó
    Note that in Figure¬†12, the line \(y=0\) was also drawn as a dashed line. This is because the values of \(y=f(x)\) will get closer and closer to zero as the inputs become more and more positive (or negative).
    üîó
    We know that the domain of this function is \((-\infty,2)\cup(2,\infty)\) as the function is undefined at \(2\text{.}\) We can determine this algebraically, and it is also evident in the graph.
    üîó
    üîó

    Remark 3.1.13.

    The line \(x=2\) in Example¬†8 is referred to as a vertical asymptote. The line \(y=0\) is referred to as a horizontal asymptote. We‚Äôll use this vocabulary when referencing such lines, but the classification of vertical asymptotes and horizontal asymptotes is beyond the scope of this book.

    Example 3.1.14.

    Algebraically find the domain of \(g(x)=\frac{3x^2}{x^2-2x-24}\text{.}\) Use technology to sketch a graph of this function.
    üîó
    Explanation.
    To find a rational function‚Äôs domain, we set the denominator equal to \(0\) and solve:
    \begin{align*} x^2-2x-24\amp=0\\ (x-6)(x+4)\amp=0 \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} x-6\amp=0\amp\text{or}\amp\amp x+4\amp=0\\ x\amp=6\amp\text{or}\amp\amp x\amp=-4 \end{align*}

    We can now begin to see what happens near \(x=-4\) and \(x=6\text{.}\) These are referred to as vertical asymptotes and will be graphed as dashed vertical lines as they are features of the graph but do not include function values.

    Example 3.1.21.

    \begin{equation*} \bar{C}(x)=\frac{30x+300000}{x}\text{.} \end{equation*}
