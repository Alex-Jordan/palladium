    Subsection 2.7.1 Imaginary Numbers

    Let‚Äôs take a fresh look at a square root with a negative radicand. Remember that the reason \(\sqrt{16}=4\) is because \(4\cdot4=16\text{.}\) So what might \(\sqrt{-16}\) mean? There is no real number that we can square to get \(-16\text{,}\) because when you square a real number, the result simply cannot be negative. You might think about \(4\) and \(-4\text{,}\) but:
    \begin{equation*} 4\cdot4=16\text{ and }(-4)(-4)=16 \end{equation*}
    so neither of those could be \(\sqrt{-16}\text{.}\) Mathematicians realized it was possible to keep track of square roots of negative numbers symbolically even though the symbols do not represent real numbers.
    üîó

    Definition 2.7.2. Imaginary Numbers.

    The imaginary unit, \(i\text{,}\) is defined by \(i=\sqrt{-1}\text{.}\) The imaginary unit satisfies the equation \(i^2=-1\text{.}\) A real number times \(i\text{,}\) such as \(4i\text{,}\) is called an imaginary number.
    üîó
    üîó
    Now we can simplify square roots with negative radicands like \(\sqrt{-16}\text{.}\)
    \begin{align*} \sqrt{-16}\amp=\sqrt{-1\cdot16}\\ \amp=\sqrt{-1}\cdot\sqrt{16}\\ \amp=i\cdot4\\ \amp=4i \end{align*}
    üîó
    Imaginary numbers are used in electrical engineering, physics, computer science, and advanced mathematics. Let‚Äôs look at some more examples.
    üîó

    Example 2.7.3.

    Simplify \(\sqrt{-2}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Explanation.
    \begin{align*} \sqrt{-2}\amp=\sqrt{-1\cdot2}\\ \amp=\sqrt{-1}\cdot\sqrt{2}\\ \amp=i\sqrt{2} \end{align*}
    We write the \(i\) in front of the radical because it can be easy to mix up \(\sqrt{2}i\) with \(\sqrt{2i}\) if you don‚Äôt draw the radical very carefully. It‚Äôs more clear as \(i\sqrt{2}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 2.7.4.

    Simplify \(\sqrt{-72}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    Explanation.
    Not only can we separate the \(\sqrt{-1}\) factor, but also in this example there is a square factor of \(72\) that can be used to simplify the expression more.
    \begin{align*} \sqrt{-72}\amp=\sqrt{-1\cdot36\cdot2}\\ \amp=\sqrt{-1}\cdot\sqrt{36}\cdot\sqrt{2}\\ \amp=6i\sqrt{2} \end{align*}
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 2.7.2 Solving Quadratic Equations with Imaginary Solutions

    Back in Example¬†2.5.9, we examined an equation that had no real solution. Let‚Äôs revisit that example now that we are aware of imaginary numbers.
    üîó

    Example 2.7.5.

    Solve for \(x\) in \(x^2+49=0\text{,}\) where \(x\) might not be a real number.
    üîó
    Explanation.
    There is no ‚Äú\(x\)‚Äù term, so we will use the square root method to solve this equation.
    \begin{align*} x^2+49\amp=0\\ x^2\amp=-49\\ x\amp=\pm\sqrt{-49}\\ x\amp=\pm\sqrt{49\cdot-1}\\ x\amp=\pm\sqrt{49}\cdot\sqrt{-1}\\ x\amp=\pm 7i \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} x\amp=-7i\amp\text{ or }\amp\amp x\amp=7i \end{align*}
    The solution set is \(\{-7i,7i\}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 2.7.6.

    Solve for \(p\) in \(p^2+75=0\text{,}\) where \(p\) might not be a real number.
    üîó
    Explanation.
    There is no ‚Äú\(p\)‚Äù term so we will use the square root method.
    \begin{align*} p^2+75\amp=0\\ p^2\amp=-75\\ p\amp=\pm\sqrt{-75}\\ p\amp=\pm\sqrt{25\cdot-1\cdot3}\\ p\amp=\pm\sqrt{25}\cdot\sqrt{-1}\cdot\sqrt{3}\\ p\amp=\pm 5i\sqrt{3} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} p\amp=-5i\sqrt{3}\amp\text{ or }\amp\amp p\amp=5i\sqrt{3} \end{align*}
    The solution set is \(\left\{-5i\sqrt{3},5i\sqrt{3}\right\}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Subsection 2.7.3 Solving Quadratic Equations with Complex Solutions

    Sometimes we need to work with a sum of a real number and an imaginary number, like \(3+2i\) or \(-4-8i\text{.}\) These combinations are called ‚Äúcomplex numbers‚Äù.
    üîó

    Definition 2.7.7. Complex Number.

    A complex number is a number that can be expressed in the form \(a + bi\text{,}\) where \(a\) and \(b\) are real numbers and \(i\) is the imaginary unit. In this expression, \(a\) is the real part and \(b\) (not \(bi\)) is the imaginary part of the complex number.
    üîó
    üîó
    Note that technically, real numbers (like \(5\)) are complex numbers too (like \(5+0i\)). And similarly, imaginary numbers (like \(7i\)) are complex numbers too (like \(0+7i\)).
    üîó

    Example 2.7.8.

    Solve for \(m\) in \((m-1)^2+18=0\text{,}\) where \(m\) might not be a real number.
    üîó
    Explanation.
    This equation has a squared expression so we will use the square root method.
    \begin{align*} (m-1)^2+18\amp=0\\ (m-1)^2\amp=-18\\ m-1\amp=\pm\sqrt{-18}\\ m-1\amp=\pm\sqrt{-1\cdot9\cdot2}\\ m-1\amp=\pm\sqrt{-1}\cdot\sqrt{9}\cdot\sqrt{2}\\ m-1\amp=\pm i\cdot3\sqrt{2}\\ m\amp=1\pm i\cdot3\sqrt{2} \end{align*}
    \begin{align*} m\amp=1-3i\sqrt{2}\amp\text{ or }\amp\amp m\amp=1+3i\sqrt{2} \end{align*}
    The solution set is \(\left\{1-3i\sqrt{2}, 1+3i\sqrt{2}\right\}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó
    üîó

    Example 2.7.9.

    In an advanced math course, you might study the relationship between a lynx polulation (or any generic predator) and a hare population (or any generic prey) as time passes. For example, if the predator population is high, they will eat many prey. But then the prey population will become low, so the predators will go hungry and have fewer offspring. With time, the predator population will decline, and that will lead to a rebound in the prey population. Then prey will be plentiful, and the preadator population will rebound, and the whole situaiton starts over. This cycle may take years or even decades to play out.
    üîó
    Strange as it may seem, to understand this phenomenon mathematically, you will need to solve equations similar to:
    \begin{equation*} (1-t)(3-t)+10=0 \end{equation*}
    Let‚Äôs practice solving this equation now.
    \begin{align*} (1-t)(3-t)+10\amp=0\\ 3-t-3t+t^2+10\amp=0\\ t^2-4t+13\amp=0 \end{align*}
    We can try the quadratic formula.
    \begin{align*} t\amp=\frac{4\pm\sqrt{(-4)^2-4(1)(13)}}{2(1)}\\ \amp=\frac{4\pm\sqrt{16-52}}{2}\\ \amp=\frac{4\pm\sqrt{-36}}{2}\\ \amp=\frac{4\pm\sqrt{-1}\cdot\sqrt{36}}{2}\\ \amp=\frac{4\pm i\cdot6}{2}\\ \amp=2\pm3i \end{align*}
    These two solutions, \(2-3i\) and \(2+3i\) have implications for how fast the predator and prey populations rise and fall over time, but you will study that in a later math course.
    üîó
    üîó
    Note that in Example¬†9, the expressions \(2+3i\) and \(2-3i\) are fully simplified. In the same way that the terms \(2\) and \(3x\) cannot be combined, the terms \(2\) and \(3i\) can not be combined.
    üîó

    Remark 2.7.10.

    Each complex solution to a quadratic equation can be checked, just as every real solution can be checked. To check the solution \(2+3i\) from Example¬†9, we can replace \(t\) with \(2+3i\) and check that the two sides of the equation are equal. In doing so, we will need to use the fact that \(i^2=-1\text{.}\) This check is shown here:
    \begin{align*} (1-t)(3-t)+10\amp=0\\ (1-\substitute{(2+3i)})(3-\substitute{(2+3i)})+10\amp\wonder{=}0\\ (1-2-3i)(3-2-3i)+10\amp\wonder{=}0\\ (-1-3i)(1-3i)+10\amp\wonder{=}0\\ -1+3i-3i+(3i)^2+10\amp\wonder{=}0\\ 9+9i^2\amp\wonder{=}0\\ 9-9\amp\confirm{=}0 \end{align*}
    üîó
    üîó
