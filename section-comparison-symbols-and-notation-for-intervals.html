    Section A.5 Comparison Symbols and Notation for Intervals

    Here is a true fact: \(8\) is larger than \(3\text{.}\) That is a comparison between two specific numbers. We can also make comparisons using an unspecified number, like if we say that average rent for an apartment in Portland, OR is more than $1700. We are not saying what the average rent is, just that it‚Äôs larger than $1700. In the first half of this section, we examine mathematical notation for making these kinds of comparisons.
    üîó
    In Oregon, only citizens \(18\) and older can vote in statewide elections. That is saying something about a large group of citizens, not just those who are \(18\text{.}\) It‚Äôs saying that people who are \(37\) and \(62\) may vote; and people who are \(12\) may not. So it‚Äôs a statement about a large collection of numbers. In the second half of this section, we examine the mathematical notation for large collections of numbers like this.
    üîó

    Subsection A.5.1 Comparison Symbols

    In everyday language you can say something like ‚Äú\(8\) is larger than \(3\)‚Äù. In mathematical writing, we have a shorthand notation for this: ‚Äú\(\gt\)‚Äù. It‚Äôs used as follows:
    \begin{equation*} 8\gt3 \end{equation*}
    That short expression is read aloud as ‚Äú\(8\) is greater than \(3\)‚Äù. The symbol ‚Äú\(\gt\)‚Äù is called the greater-than symbol.
    üîó

    Remark A.5.3.

    At some point in history, it was settled that ‚Äú\(\gt\)‚Äù was a good symbol for ‚Äúis greater than‚Äù. The tall side of the symbol is with the larger of the two numbers, and the small pointed side is with the smaller of the two numbers. One way to remember how this symbol works is to imagine it as an open mouth, and tell yourself that the mouth is hungry and it wants to eat the larger number.
    üîó
    üîó
    We have to be careful when negative numbers are used in a comparison. Is \(-8\) greater or less than \(-3\text{?}\) In one sense \(-8\) is larger, because if you owe someone \(8\) dollars, that‚Äôs ‚Äúmore‚Äù than owing them \(3\) dollars. But the ‚Äú\(\gt\)‚Äù symbol does not work that way. This symbol tells you which number is farther to the right on a number line. With that understanding, \(-3\) is greater than \(-8\text{.}\)
    üîó

    And so we see the answer is \({8>4.6>{\frac{10}{3}}>3.14159>-5.2}\text{.}\)

    The greater-than symbol has a close relative: the greater-than-or-equal-to symbol ‚Äú\(\geq\)‚Äù. It means just like it sounds; the left number is either greater than or equal to the right number. Consider these examples, five of which are true and one of which is false.
    \begin{align*} 8\amp\geq3\amp3\amp\geq-8\amp3\amp\geq3\\ 8\amp\gt3\amp3\amp\gt-8\amp3\amp\reject{\gt}3 \end{align*}
    üîó
    While it may seem unhelpful to write \(3\geq3\) when you could write \(3=3\text{,}\) the ‚Äú\(\geq\)‚Äù symbol is useful when at least one of the numbers in a comparison is not specific, like in these examples:
    \begin{align*} (\text{hourly pay rate})\amp\geq(\text{minimum wage})\amp(\text{age of a voter})\amp\geq18 \end{align*}
    üîó
    Sometimes you want to emphasize that one number is less than another number. For this, we have symbols that are reversed from \(\gt\) and \(\geq\text{.}\) The symbol ‚Äú\(\lt\)‚Äù is the less-than symbol and it‚Äôs used like this:
    \begin{equation*} 3\lt8 \end{equation*}
    and read aloud as ‚Äú\(3\) is less than \(8\)‚Äù.
    üîó
    Table¬†6 gives the complete list of all six comparison symbols. We‚Äôve only discussed three of them so far in this section, but you already know the equals symbol. The other two are the ‚Äúless than or equal to‚Äù symbol, ‚Äú\(\leq\)‚Äù, and the ‚Äúnot equal to‚Äù symbol, ‚Äú\(\neq\)‚Äù.
    üîó

    Subsection A.5.2 Set-Builder and Interval Notation

    If you write
    \begin{equation*} (\text{age of a voter})\geq18 \end{equation*}
    and have a particular voter in mind, what is that person‚Äôs age? Maybe they are \(18\text{,}\) but maybe they are older. It‚Äôs helpful to use a variable \(a\) to represent age (in years) and then to visualize the possibilities with a number line.
    üîó
    The shaded portion of the number line in Figure¬†7 is a mathematical interval. That means a collection of certain numbers with a ‚Äústarting point‚Äù and a ‚Äúending point‚Äù. The interval above doesn‚Äôt really ever end, but we can say \(\infty\) (infinity) is the ‚Äúending point‚Äù in this situation. So this interval starts at \(18\) and ‚Äúends‚Äù at \(\infty\text{.}\)
    üîó
    The number line in Figure¬†7 is a visual representation of a collection of certain numbers. We have notations we can use to write down such collections of numbers.
    üîó

    Definition A.5.8. Set-Builder Notation.

    Set-builder notation attempts to say directly what condition needs to be met by numbers in the interval. We write set-builder notation like:
    \begin{equation*} \left\{x\mid\text{condition on }x\right\} \end{equation*}
    and read it aloud as ‚Äúthe set of all \(x\) such that ‚Ä¶‚Äù.
    üîó
    üîó
    For example, \(\left\{x\mid x\geq18\right\}\) is read aloud as ‚Äúthe set of all \(x\) such that \(x\) is greater than or equal to \(18\)‚Äù. The breakdown is as follows.
    üîó
    \(\highlight{\{}x\mid x\geq18\highlight{\}}\) the set of
    \(\{\highlight{x}\mid x\geq18\}\) all \(x\)
    \(\{x\highlight{{}\mid{}}x\geq18\}\) such that
    \(\{x\mid\highlight{x\geq18}\}\) \(x\) is greater than or equal to \(18\)

    Example A.5.9.

    The set of all positive numbers is \(\{x\mid x\gt0\}\text{,}\) but the set of all non-negative numbers is \(\{x\mid x\geq0\}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    The set of all possible Celsius temperatures for liquid water is \(\{x\mid x\gt0\text{ and }x\lt100\}\text{.}\)
    üîó
    üîó

    Set-builder notation is useful, but there is an alternative for intervals that is less cumbersome.
    üîó

    Definition A.5.11. Interval Notation.

    Interval notation describes an interval by saying where it ‚Äústarts‚Äù and ‚Äúends‚Äù. It can look like any of these four options:
    \begin{equation*} (\text{start},\text{end})\qquad (\text{start},\text{end}]\qquad [\text{start},\text{end})\qquad [\text{start},\text{end}] \end{equation*}
    üîó
    üîó
    In Figure¬†7, the interval starts at \(18\text{.}\) Then it extends forever and has no end, so we use the \(\infty\) symbol for where this interval ‚Äúends‚Äù. And we write \([18,\infty)\text{.}\) There is a subtlety about using the bracket ‚Äú\([\)‚Äù on one side and the parenthesis ‚Äú\()\)‚Äù on the other side. The bracket tells us that \(18\) is part of the interval and the parenthesis tells us that \(\infty\) is not part of the interval.
    üîó
    Imagine if we wanted to describe all the numbers greater than \(18\text{,}\) including numbers like \(18.01\) but not including \(18\) itself. Then we would write \((18,\infty)\text{.}\)
    üîó
    So there are four types of infinite intervals. Take note of the different uses of round parentheses and square brackets.
    üîó

    Remark A.5.14. Alternative Convention for Sketching Intervals.

    When graphing an interval, there is an alternative convention than you might see in other resources explaining algebra. This other convention uses open circles and filled-in circles. An open circle is used in place of a parenthesis, and a filled-in circle is used in place of a bracket, as in this example for the interval \([a,b)\text{.}\)
